Computer Vision Research - Caeli

Teaching Machines to Count Trees From Space

February 2024 - July 2024 | Research Internship

Joined a research project that's using AI to track deforestation in real-time. The goal? Build computer vision models that can automatically detect and count trees in satellite imagery. It's like playing "Where's Waldo" but with forests, and the stakes are saving the planet.

What I Actually Did

  • Built and trained deep learning models using PyTorch and TensorFlow
  • Processed thousands of satellite images to teach AI what trees look like from space
  • Optimized models to run fast enough for real-world use (turns out speed matters when you're monitoring entire forests)
  • Created automated pipelines that can process new satellite data without human intervention
  • Wrote documentation that other researchers can actually understand and use

The Cool Technical Stuff

We used state-of-the-art object detection architectures, fine-tuned them on custom datasets, and got them running efficiently enough to process real satellite feeds. The tricky part wasn't just detecting trees - it was distinguishing individual tree canopies from each other in dense forests.

Why This Matters

Traditional forest monitoring takes months and requires teams of people manually analyzing images. Our AI can do the same analysis in hours and catch deforestation as it happens, not months later. It's environmental protection with a tech upgrade.

What I Learned

Working on research taught me that building AI for real-world problems is completely different from following online tutorials. You spend most of your time on data quality, edge cases, and making sure your model actually works when it matters.

University Thesis Project

AI Tree Detection Showdown

My final thesis project: comparing three different AI models to see which one is best at detecting individual trees in aerial photographs. Think of it as a heavyweight championship, but for computer vision algorithms that specialize in forest analysis.

The Research Question

  • Built a custom dataset of annotated tree images from various aerial sources (turns out manually marking thousands of trees is as tedious as it sounds)
  • Implemented three competing approaches: Mask R-CNN, YOLACT, and a custom YOLO-based solution
  • Tested each model on speed, accuracy, and how well they handle edge cases like overlapping tree canopies
  • Benchmarked performance across different image qualities and forest types

Making It Practical

  • Integrated the best-performing model with QGIS so forestry professionals could actually use it
  • Created automated pipelines that process raw aerial imagery and output georeferenced tree maps
  • Built it to work with real-world data, not just clean academic datasets

What I Discovered

  • The "best" model depends entirely on what you're optimizing for - speed vs accuracy vs computational resources
  • Data quality matters more than model complexity (garbage in, garbage out, even with fancy AI)
  • Academic papers often skip the messy parts of making AI work in production environments

The Real Contribution

Beyond comparing models, I created a reproducible workflow that other researchers can use and improve upon. The QGIS integration means forestry professionals can use this technology without needing a computer science degree.
